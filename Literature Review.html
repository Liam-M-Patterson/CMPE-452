Abstract:
Drivable area or free space detection is an important task in Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) and autonomous driving system. It can help intelligent vehicles understand road conditions and determine safe driving area. Semantic segmentation is a pixel-wise prediction which can classify each pixel into its category. In this paper, we propose a deep learning-based semantic segmentation architecture to predict the drivable area in front of the vehicle. Our model is built based on ResNet backbone with the Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) and Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) modules. The backbone in the bottom-up architecture extracts features and an ASPP is attached to the last decoder layer. Additionally, a top-down architecture with lateral connections is added in the decoder and the FPN utilizes the multi-scale features for final prediction. Our model is evaluated on the Cityscapes street scene dataset and achieves 95.90% mIoU on road segmentation. Next, the model is evaluated on the BDD100K large-scale diverse driving dataset with direct drivable region and alternative drivable region annotations. For this dataset our model achieves 84.58% mIoU which is comparable to some State-of-the-Art models.

SECTION I.

Introduction

With the rapid development of science and technology, auto industry has been making great progress in autonomous driving technology. While the industry is striving to achieve fully autonomous vehicles, researchers are constantly developing driving assistance systems to enhance a safer driving environment and a more pleasant driving experience. Advance Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) such as the lane keeping system, makes it easy to drive on a road with clear lane marking [1]. However, when we drive, we not only depend on lane markings, but also need to interact with other objects and plan route based on our own purpose. For example, people can distinguish drivable areas without lane markings, adjust the speed to keep a safe distance from vehicles in front, and occasionally switch lanes to pass other vehicles. Therefore, it is crucial to understand the complex driving environment and determine the drivable region in real-time.

Fig. 1 displays several examples of driving scenes and the corresponding drivable area segmentation annotations from the BDD100K [2] dataset. An ego vehicle has a video camera in the front of the vehicle to collect the traffic information. The drivable area can be divided into two different categories based on its trajectories: the direct drivable area and the alternative drivable area. Direct drivable area, marked in red, means the ego vehicle has the priority to keep driving in this area. Alternative drivable area, marked in blue, means the ego vehicle can drive in the area, but has to be cautious since based on the regulation, other vehicles may have priority access to the area.

Drivable area detection is challenging since it not only requires road detection, but also requires analysis of the traffic information such as the position of the traffic components to identify whether the area can be safely driven on. Drivable area can be detected with different methods. First, classical computer vision methods utilize the exterior features including color, shape and topography in 2D images [23], [24]. Second, joint 2D image and 3D sensor methods have been proposed to leverage the 3D information for drivable area detection [25]–[27]. Finally, deep learning-based semantic image segmentation architectures have shown great success recently in detecting drivable area efficiently [30], [31], and can extract deeper hierarchical features using a video camera.

In recent years, owing to its high feature extraction ability, accuracy and efficiency, deep learning models with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have proven to be a successful method in solving image classification [5], [6], object detection [7], semantic segmentation [12]–[17], [19]–[21] problems. Semantic segmentation is a pixel-wise labeling technique which can classify each pixel into its category and help understand the images at the pixel-level. Meanwhile, because of the availability of the public benchmark traffic scene datasets such as BDD100K [2], Cityscapes [3] and KITTI [4], we can train and evaluate deep learning models. The existing models have achieved great performance on traffic image segmentation and traffic scene parsing [19] and can be leveraged to detect drivable areas in autonomous vehicles.

In this paper, we leverage the encoder-decoder deep learning structure to analyze driving scenes and predict drivable areas for the ego vehicle using a video camera. A pre-trained ResNet [5] is applied as the backbone network to extract features from the input images. Then, the Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) module is added to the last encoder layer to gather contextual information and more semantic information. A Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) is built to leverage strong representation of features from low to high levels. The final predicted feature map is reconstructed from the feature maps of all scales.

Two datasets including Cityscapes street scene dataset [3] and the BDD100K large-scale driving dataset [2] are applied to evaluate the effectiveness of our drivable area detection architecture. The Cityscapes dataset includes multiple classes of annotations such as road, person, car and vegetation. Only the road class is utilized to evaluate our model on road segmentation based on the traffic scene information, and the model should have the ability to only predict the drivable road. Our model achieves 98.03% Pixel Accuracy (PA), 98.00% mean Pixel Accuracy (mPA) and 95.90% mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) on the Cityscapes dataset. The BDD100K dataset is more challenging as it has more complex and strict requirements. It not only requires segmenting the road based on the traffic information, but also distinguishing the direct drivable region and alternative drivable region in the traffic scenes. Therefore, the model should have the ability to analyze the traffic lanes and geological information of different traffic participants, and then utilize the deeper semantic information to predict different drivable areas from the input traffic images. Our model achieves 97.09% PA, 91.14% mPA and 84.58% mIoU respectively on the BDD100K drivable area detection validation dataset.

The paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the related works on semantic segmentation, multi-scale feature networks and drivable area detection models. Our methodology and experimental results are illustrated in Section III and Section IV respectively. Section V concludes the paper with a list of future work on drivable area detection.

SECTION II.

Related Work

A. Semantic Segmentation

Semantic segmentation is an important task in image processing. CNNs have proven to be the most successful method in solving this problem. Fully Convolutional Networks (FCNs) [12] replace the fully-connected layers with convolutional layers. The output is segmentation feature map instead of classification scores. The last layer can reconstruct the encoded feature maps into the original image size with predicted class probabilities. Almost all recent semantic segmentation models are based on FCNs with an encode-decode structure [12]–[17].

Semantic segmentation is a pixel-wise prediction that relies on contextual relationship and global information. U-Net [13] and its variations [14], [15] are commonly used for biomedical image segmentation. The skip connections transfer encoded feature maps to decoder layers, which enables the model to have multi-level feature representations for the final prediction. Qiao et al. [14] applied stacked dilated convolution [22] module and Chen et al. [20] proposed atrous convolution to enlarge the receptive fields of the networks and gather the contextual information. Dilated convolution and atrous convolution both introduce a new parameter to the convolution layers called dilation rate or atrous rate. It defines the amount of spacing between the values in a kernel and equals to the number of sampling strides on the input feature map. The receptive field of the kernel increases as the dilation rate is increased.

Zhao et al. (PSPNet) [19] and Chen et al. (DeepLab) [20], [21] utilize Spatial Pyramid Pooling (SPP) and Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) which aim at obtaining more global context for semantic segmentation. The SPP module in PSPNet fuses four feature maps of different scales with different pooling strides to gather context information. Chen et al. [21] replace the SPP module by using atrous convolution with different atrous rates. Similarly, we apply dilated/atrous convolution after the last encoder block to utilize the contextual information of multiple scales.

B. Feature Networks

Feature networks are added to the feature extraction backbone to utilize the feature representations of different scales. The bottom-up path is a CNN backbone which extracts pyramidal feature hierarchy with feature maps in multiple scales. A top-down path receives feature maps from higher pyramid levels by using skip connections or lateral connections. Lin et al. [16] and Pinheiro et al. [17] add a top-down path and a refinement module in order to build one high resolution feature map for semantic segmentation. Lin et al. [8] utilize feature maps from all levels of top-down path which is called Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) to make predictions at multi-levels. Path Aggregation Network (PANet) [9] adds an extra augmented bottom-up path to propagate low-level features for instance segmentation. Some other FPNs such as NAS-FPN [10] and BiFPN [11], are designed to improve the accuracy and efficiency of image processing.

C. Drivable Area Detection

Traditional lane detection models can localize the lanes in front of an ego vehicle, while drivable area detection models can distinguish drivable area on the street. Gao et al. [23] utilize the color cues from consecutive frames and detect drivable area in different conditions such as normal road, shadowed road and marked road. Yao et al. [24] identify drivable area with Support Vector Machine (SVM) and achieve 82.51% F1-score on KITTI dataset [4]. The methods utilize lower-level features and can detect drivable area in less occluded area, but are vulnerable in complicated traffic environment. A combination of 2D features from segmentation networks and 3D structure from Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) is applied by Patra et al. [25] for road segmentation. Lidar data and video data are applied in [26], [27], while Liu et al. [26] mainly rely on color and Li et al. [27] apply multi-features. Tsutsui et al. [29] apply weakly supervised learning to reduce the cost of manual annotations. Scheck et al. [30] use encoder-decoder structure to detect drivable area with a fisheye camera and obtain 90.7% mIoU on Cityscapes dataset with the road annotations. Pizzati et al. [31] propose a multi-tasks encoder-decoder network to predict drivable area and traffic scene together on BDD100K dataset and achieves 82.62% mIoU.

SECTION III.

Methodology

In this section, we introduce our drivable area detection architecture. Both road segmentation and drivable area detection tasks require the model to extract traffic semantic information, and analyze the traffic participants such as vehicles and pedestrians from the traffic scene images. Our semantic segmentation model is an encoder-decoder structure. Based on this structure, we added two modules to mine the richer and deeper semantic information from the traffic scenes. Our overall model architecture is shown in Fig. 2. The model consists of three main parts including: i) an ImageNet pretrained ResNet backbone network is applied to extract the deep features from the input traffic images, ii) an Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) module is added to the end of the encoder to extract rich semantic information, and iii) a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) module is applied to collect rich feature information from different scales of feature maps.

A. Encoder Backbone Network

The backbone of our model is ResNet [5] with 101 convolution layers, and its corresponding ImageNet-pretrained weights. As shown in Fig. 2, the ResNet backbone has 5 residual blocks B0, B1, B2, B3 and B4 with a scaling step of 2. The feature representations of the last 4 blocks have strides of 4, 8, 16, 32 respectively, which are applied in a subsequent decoder network and a FPN module. Configuration of the ResNet architecture is shown in Table I with input resolution of 720 × 1280 pixels.

B. Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP)

After the last encoder (B4), ASPP module from [21] is applied to extract the contextual features in the last decoder feature map. The ASPP module fuses five different feature maps together. The first one is obtained by global pooling to generate a single bin output. The second one is the output of a 1 × 1 convolution layer. The last three parts are 3×3 convolution layers with different dilation rates of 4, 8 and 16. Then, these five feature maps are concatenated together and a 1 × 1 convolution layer is applied to reduce the number of channels.

TABLE I: Configuration of the ResNet101 Backbone Architecture

C. Decoder and Feature Pyramid Network (FPN)

The outputs of the last encoder blocks B1, B2, B3, B4 are transferred to the corresponding decoder blocks F1, F2, F3, F4. 1×1 convolution layers are added to the lateral connections to reduce the number of channels. Upsampling by a factor of 2 is applied to the decoders’ outputs, and the upsampled features are merged with the encoders’ outputs as shown in Fig. 2.

The 4 output feature maps learned from different levels are upsampled to transform them to the same size with scale factors of 4, 8, 16, 32 respectively, and passed through a 3×3 convolution layers as shown in Fig. 2. Finally, the multi-scaled feature maps are concatenated together and up sampled to the original size with a factor of 4. Two 3×3 convolution layers are applied to refine the up sampled pyramid architecture and one 1 × 1 convolution layer is leveraged to reconstruct the prediction feature map. The number of channels of the last output layer is equal to the number of classes.

SECTION IV.

Experiments

In this section, we present the evaluation results of our drivable area detection model using the Cityscapes dataset [3] and the BDD100K dataset [2].

A. Implementation Details

Our model is implemented using the PyTorch framework, trained and evaluated on Tesla V100 GPU having 32 GB RAM, CUDA v10.1 and cuDNN v9.1. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimizer with a momentum of 0.9 and weight decay of 0.001 is applied to train the network.

B. Evaluation Metrics

We apply Pixel Accuracy (PA), Mean Pixel Accuracy (mPA) and Mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) to measure the effectiveness of our model.

C. Cityscapes - Road Segmentation

In order to test the performance of our semantic segmentation model in understanding traffic scenes, we first use the Cityscapes [3] dataset which contains recorded street scenes from 50 different cities. It consists of 5,000 image frames with high quality pixel-level annotations of 30 classes for evaluation including road, sidewalk, car, bus, traffic light, person and sky. Since the goal is to detect the drivable area on the road, we only use the road annotations in the Cityscapes dataset thereby it has two classes: background and drivable area. The images are split into three sets with numbers 2,750, 500 and 1,525 for training, validation and testing respectively. Several road ground truth annotations are displayed in Fig. 3 (b).

We train with ResNet101 backbone with ASPP and FPN modules and obtain 95.90% mIoU on the road segmentation. The results are shown in Table II and several prediction instances are displayed in Fig. 3 (c).

D. BDD100K - Drivable Area Detection

The Cityscapes dataset only provides the road annotation, while the drivable area detection dataset of the BDD100K dataset [2] divided the drivable area into directly drivable area and alternatively drivable area. Therefore, to detect the drivable area, there are three classes including the background. It is a challenging dataset since the videos include diverse scene types (city streets, tunnels, residential areas, parking and highways), collected under different weather conditions (clear, overcast, snowy, rainy, cloudy and foggy) at different times of the day. The drivable area detection dataset contains 100,000 high resolution (720 × 1, 280 pixels) traffic scene images with corresponding drivable area annotations. The dataset is split into training (70,000), validation (10,000) and testing (20,000) sets.

SECTION V.

Conclusions

Drivable area detection is a crucial task in ADAS and fully autonomous vehicles. It can help vehicles run safely on the road and plan route efficiently. We apply deep learning-based image segmentation network to predict drivable area on the street including direct drivable area and alternative drivable area. ASPP and FPN modules are utilized to improve the model’s perceptive field. We evaluate the model on the Cityscapes dataset with road annotation and achieve 98.00% mAP and 95.90% mIoU. Finally, our model is trained on BDD100K drivable area detection dataset and achieve 91.14% mAP and 84.58% mIoU on the validation dataset which are greater than the baseline ResNet101 model.

Future research will focus on leveraging the detected drivable area for trajectory planning. Additionally, the drivable area detection cannot identify the potential risks such as the vision is obstructed by other obstacles, so vehicular communications will be applied to enable safe driving for autonomous vehicles in real life traffic scenario.
